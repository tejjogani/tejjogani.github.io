The playbook

How this system thinks

Four chapters: what regimes are, how strategies are judged, how ideas become evidence, and how evidence becomes positions.

One rule governs everything here: the model never sees the future. Every signal below is computed "walk-forward" — at each month-end, using only information available on that date, exactly as you would have experienced it. Backtests that skip this rule (most of them) produce beautiful curves and real losses.

1 · Market regimes — the weather system

Markets don't have one personality. They alternate between calm grinds and violent storms, and the storms cluster.

In plain words

The market has moods: calm, jumpy, panicked. You can't predict which way prices go tomorrow — but you can tell what mood the market is in today, and the mood tells you how violent tomorrow is likely to be. Every risk decision in this playbook starts there. In twenty years around institutional money I never met a desk that could reliably call direction; every good one I met sized positions by the weather.

What a hidden Markov model actually is

Two words, two assumptions. Markov: the market's state tomorrow depends only on its state today — like weather, a stormy day is most likely followed by another stormy day, regardless of how long ago summer was. Hidden: you never observe the state directly. Nobody rings a bell announcing "stress regime" — you see only what the state emits: each day's return and volatility. The model's job is to reason backwards from the visible emissions to the invisible state.

Concretely, ours has three moving parts:

1 · Three states, each with its own statistical fingerprint. Each state is defined by what returns look like while the market is in it. Here is the actual fitted model as of 2026-07-23:

3 · Inference — each day the model runs a Bayes update (the "forward algorithm"): start from yesterday's state probabilities, push them through the transition matrix, then reweight by how well each state explains today's return and volatility.

Reading the matrix, cell by cell

Each row is "given today's mood…", each column is "…the chance of tomorrow's mood." Rows sum to 100% because tomorrow has to be something. Three cells deserve your attention:

  • The diagonal (calm→calm 98.1%, mid→mid 96.1%, stress→stress 98.0%): moods overwhelmingly continue. This is the entire economic value of the model. A handy formula: if a state's stay-probability is p, its typical visit lasts 1/(1−p) days — that's how the "~53 days" column is computed (1/(1−0.981) ≈ 53).
  • Calm→stress = 0.000. In 33 years of data, the market has essentially never jumped from sunshine straight to hurricane in one day. It walks down a staircase: calm → mid → stress. That means the mid state is your early-warning zone — by the time stress arrives, you were already watching.
  • Stress→calm = 0.000 too: storms don't end abruptly either. Recovery also goes through mid. Symmetric staircase.

One day of the actual math

Here's a full Bayes update by hand, using today's real numbers. Write yesterday's belief as a probability vector across (calm, mid, stress) — currently roughly (36%, 64%, 0%).

Step 1 — push through the matrix (what do we expect before seeing today?). Each new entry is "sum over yesterday's states × their transition chance." For stress: 36%×0.000 + 64%×0.015 + 0%×0.980 ≈ 1%. Doing all three gives the prior: (37%, 62%, 1%).

Step 2 — score the evidence. Each state is a bell curve over daily returns with its own width: calm's daily σ is ≈0.56% (that's 8.9% annual ÷ √252 — volatility scales with the square root of time), mid's ≈0.96%, stress's ≈1.7%. Now suppose today drops −2.5%. How surprising is that to each state? Measure in σ-units: z = −2.5/0.56 ≈ −4.5σ for calm (a once-per-decades event — calm is shocked), −2.6σ for mid (rare but real), −1.5σ for stress (a Tuesday). The bell-curve heights at those points are the likelihoods, roughly in ratio 0.01 : 3.6 : 20.

Step 3 — multiply and renormalize. Posterior ∝ prior × likelihood: calm 37%×0.01, mid 62%×3.6, stress 1%×20 → rescale to sum to 100% → (0.1%, 92%, 8%).

Read what just happened: one violent day took stress from 1% to only 8% — the model stays skeptical, because the prior said stress was unlikely and calm markets do have bad days. But run the same arithmetic tomorrow: the prior for stress now starts near 9%, so a second wild day lifts it to ~40%, and a third makes it the leading hypothesis. That's the machine in one sentence: priors keep it from panicking at noise; accumulating likelihoods force it to accept a storm within a few days. Exactly the temperament you want in a risk manager.

How it's fitted, and how we keep it honest

Fitting uses expectation-maximization (Baum–Welch): guess the states, infer which days belong to which state, re-estimate each state's fingerprint from its days, repeat until stable. Nothing is labeled by hand — the model discovers "calm/mid/stress" on its own, purely because three clusters of behavior genuinely exist in the data. Two disciplines keep it honest: it is refit only once a year on an expanding window (no future data ever), and states are ranked by volatility after each refit so "stress" always means the same thing even if the model reorders its internal labels.

Why this matters for money

Look at the persistence column: regimes last weeks to months, not days. That's the entire trade. Because volatility clusters, knowing today's state genuinely tells you about tomorrow's risk — the stress state's volatility is several times calm's, and once entered it sticks around. You can't predict returns from this table (note the return column's noisiness), but you can predict the width of what might happen — and position size should scale with width. The limits are just as important: the model recognizes storms slightly late (it needs evidence to accumulate), it says nothing about why a storm started, and it will occasionally flicker at boundaries — which is why the playbook demands a second, independent signal (credit, trend) before acting hard.

Why credit leads stocks — the transmission chain

This is the single most important mechanism in the playbook, so it's worth the full walk-through. Corporate America runs on rolling debt: companies don't pay loans off, they refinance them — old bonds mature, new bonds are issued to repay them. That treadmill only works while lenders are willing.

Now consider who notices trouble first. A stockholder owns unlimited upside, so hope is part of the position. A bondholder's best case is only getting their money back — bond investors are paid to imagine the downside, and nothing else. When the economy's data starts smelling wrong, they act first, and they act on the riskiest borrowers first: junk-rated companies, the ones with no cushion. Those borrowers' bond prices sag, meaning the yield lenders demand over safe Treasuries — the spread — widens. All of this happens in the bond market's pricing months before weak demand shows up in any earnings report, because it's a change in willingness-to-lend, not a change in reported results.

Then the mechanical damage arrives in three steps. One: companies refinancing at higher rates see interest expense eat their earnings. Two: the weakest can't refinance at all — defaults begin, layoffs follow, the fear feeds itself. Three (subtlest, biggest): every asset's price is a claim on future cash flows discounted back to today, and when the price of money rises, the discount is harsher — so valuations compress even for companies whose businesses are fine.

Here is why the conjunction with valuations is the bubble-burst fingerprint. Expensive markets are, mathematically, markets whose price depends mostly on distant future cash flows — which makes them maximally sensitive to that discounting step. Cheap markets can absorb tighter money; expensive ones cannot. High CAPE alone predicts almost nothing about the next year (expensive markets happily got more expensive for years — 1997, 1998, 1999, 2024, 2025…). Tight credit alone is survivable when assets are cheap. But expensive assets meeting expensive money removes the escape routes: 2000 (spreads had been widening since the 1998 LTCM shock while the Nasdaq doubled), 2007–08 (high-yield cracked in June 2007; the S&P peaked that October, months later), 2022 (the Fed repriced money and the longest-duration assets — unprofitable tech — fell hardest). Same fingerprint, three different decades.

The one-line version

Stocks are priced on hope, bonds on fear — and fear does its homework earlier. Watch what lenders charge the weakest borrowers: it's the market's smoke detector, and it goes off before the flames reach the earnings statements. When it fires while valuations are stretched, history's worst drawdowns follow. That pairing — not either signal alone — is the edge this system was built to test.

2 · The strategy lab — judging rules honestly

Four rules, same 18-year window (July 2008 → today), monthly rebalance, signals applied to the following month. Cash earns 0% — a deliberate handicap against the timing rules.

In plain words

We pretend to trade each rule through 18 years of history, never letting it see the future, and score three things: what it made, how rough the ride was, and how deep the worst hole got. The finding — and this matches every serious study since the 1970s — is that simple timing rules don't beat the market; they make the crashes survivable. That's a product worth owning, as long as you know that's what you bought.

The three numbers that matter

CAGR — compound annual growth, "what you actually made per year." Sharpe — return per unit of volatility; how much sleep each percent cost (0.8 decent, 1.0 good for a simple rule). Max drawdown — the worst peak-to-trough fall; the number that decides whether you'd really have stayed in the seat.

The honest result: the filters gave up ~2.4% CAGR and halved the worst loss. That's not a defect — it's the actual product. You are buying insurance with expected return. Whether that trade is worth it depends on account size, temperament, and when you'll need the money — which is why it's a decision to make in calm markets, in advance.

3 · The relationship graph — ideas on trial

Indicators are nodes. Hypotheses about how they combine are edges. Every edge must earn its status from walk-forward evidence — and rejected edges are kept, because remembering what doesn't work is half the edge. Click anything.

In plain words

Every market belief gets written as "IF this, THEN that," tested against history, and stamped with a verdict. Funds pay research teams millions to maintain exactly this: an institutional memory of what's been tried. Ours is a JSON file and a nightly script — but the discipline is identical, and the discipline is the moat.

The most valuable negative result in finance

No edge cleared statistical significance (|t| > 2) for predicting average returns — with only 34–75 stress-months in the sample, noisy means can't clear that bar. Yet the strategies built on the same signals clearly cut drawdowns. Both are true because of a cornerstone empirical fact: returns are nearly unpredictable; risk is very predictable. Volatility under stress conditions runs 6–8% a month versus ~5% baseline, and it clusters. The signals don't say "the market will fall" — they say "if it falls, it falls harder." De-risking on them is defense on variance, not a forecast of direction.

4 · Companies that move together

Take 64 stocks, one year of daily returns, and ask a single question: who actually trades together? No sector labels, no industry codes — just correlation. Drag your eyes around; click any node.

In plain words

We let the computer group stocks purely by who rises and falls together. It rediscovers the industry map on its own — chips with chips, banks with banks — which proves the groupings are real money flows, not opinions. Then the question that makes money: which group is the market's cash actually crowding into, and does the news story agree? When flows and story agree you have a trend; when they disagree, one of them is lying.

What just happened

The clusters above were discovered by label propagation: every stock starts as its own group, then repeatedly adopts the most common group among its correlated neighbors until the network settles. Nobody told it what a semiconductor is — yet it rebuilt the sector map (chips, banks, pharma, energy, payments) from co-movement alone. That's the proof the structure is real, not imposed.

How to read it against the news

The graph tells you what is moving together; the news tells you why. The discipline is to require both:

  • Trend + story = trustworthy. The top cluster right now is chips at +24.7% over three months — and the narrative (AI capex) is on every front page. Co-movement confirms the story is actually being expressed in prices, together, across the group.
  • Story without co-movement = suspect. If the headlines scream a theme but the supposed beneficiaries aren't actually correlating, the market doesn't believe it. Fade the narrative.
  • Co-movement without a story = investigate. A cluster tightening or a stock migrating between clusters before any news is often the market pricing something not yet public. That migration is one of the oldest quiet signals there is.
  • Diversification is cluster-level, not ticker-level. Owning three names in one cluster is one bet, three times. Check which clusters your holdings actually live in — the ring markers make it visible.

5 · Longs, shorts, and staying flat

The point of understanding the market is to act intelligently. Here's how evidence maps to the three possible actions.

In plain words

Three possible positions: own stocks (long), own fewer or none (flat), bet on a fall (short). Owning is the default because markets drift up over time — you're paid for enduring the swings. Going flat needs only proof that the storm risk rose (we have that proof). Shorting needs proof prices will actually fall — a far higher bar that our evidence doesn't clear, and the graveyard of every smart person who confused "overvalued" with "about to go down."

Long is the default, not a prediction

Across the sample the market rose in about 64% of months, averaging +1.04%/month — that upward drift is the base rate, the payment for bearing risk. So a long position doesn't need a bullish forecast; it needs the absence of tested warnings. That's why the dashboard shows warning lights, not buy signals.

Flat (de-risking) needs only a variance edge

To justify cutting exposure you don't need to predict a fall — you only need evidence that the distribution got wider, and that's exactly what the regime and credit nodes deliver. This is the cheapest edge in markets because it's the most reliable one.

Shorting needs a direction edge — and we don't have one yet

A short fights the drift: the market's +1%/month base rate is a headwind you pay every month you're early, on top of borrow costs, and your maximum loss is unbounded. Look at the graph's evidence: even our scariest tested condition — credit stress and broken trend together — still averaged +0.8%/month. Historically, being short in those months lost money on average. The bar for an outright short is an edge with a genuinely negative conditional mean and |t| > 2. Nothing in the graph clears it. What professionals do instead of naked shorts: reduce exposure, buy puts (defined risk, known cost), or go long/short pairs where the market drift cancels out. If the CAPE × credit edge validates with a negative mean once the valuation node is wired in, that becomes this playbook's first legitimate short signal — and that test is precisely why it's next on the roadmap.

The asymmetry to remember

De-risking needs an edge in variance (easy to find, we have it). Shorting needs an edge in direction (rare, we don't — yet). Confusing the two is how people turn a good storm detector into a losing short book.

6 · Sentiment, Reddit, and the new era of data

What billion-dollar funds actually do with alternative data, what internet noise is and isn't worth, and exactly how sentiment will plug into our graph and regime model.

In plain words

Big funds stopped waiting for earnings reports years ago — they buy credit-card data, satellite photos of parking lots, app-download counts, and run language models over every earnings call, all to estimate the numbers before they're announced. Reddit and Twitter are the noisiest end of that spectrum: nearly useless for "which way will the stock go," genuinely useful for "how crowded and fragile is this trade." We'll treat internet sentiment the way we treat everything: a node in the graph that has to earn a verdict.

The hierarchy of information

Keep the order straight, because it's the order the money respects. First: cash flows — earnings, margins, guidance. That's what a stock is. Second: market dynamics — the regime, credit conditions, who's crowded into what (chapters 1–4). Third: sentiment and attention — which never changes what a company is worth, but changes who is holding it and how firmly. Sentiment is a conditioning variable: it tells you how violently the first two will get repriced when they change.

What the research actually says about internet noise

  • Attention predicts buying, and that buying predicts poor returns. The classic Barber–Odean result: retail investors buy what grabbed their attention, that surge pushes prices past fair value, and forward returns after attention spikes are below average. An attention spike is closer to a sell signal than a buy signal.
  • Sentiment works best as a contrarian signal at extremes. Euphoria in a crowded name means the marginal buyer is already in — nobody's left to buy. Panic means the marginal seller is done. Mid-range sentiment tells you nothing.
  • The half-life is days, not months. Viral sentiment decays fast, which fights our monthly cadence. What survives at longer horizons is crowding: how one-sided the positioning got.
  • Survivorship bias is the trap. You remember the GameStop that 100×'d, not the hundred tickers that got the same treatment and bled out. Any test we run must include the ones that didn't work.

How it wires into our architecture

Into the graph first, not the HMM. The regime model's inputs need decades of history and stable meaning — clean Reddit data barely covers one full cycle, and grafting it in would change what the states mean. So sentiment enters the same way credit did: as new nodes with testable edges. Concretely, using Google Trends (2004→) as the long-history attention proxy plus Reddit/Stocktwits mention counts:

  • Node: attention z-score per ticker (how abnormal is this week's search/mention volume vs its own history).
  • Edge to test: "attention spike → next-quarter underperformance" (the Barber–Odean effect, on our universe, walk-forward).
  • Edge to test: "cluster-level euphoria + stretched valuation → weak forward returns" — the sentiment version of the credit conjunction, and a direct read on today's AI-chips crowding.
  • Edge to test: "attention spike on a name migrating between co-movement clusters → volatility event ahead" — connecting chapter 4's network to the sentiment node.

Graduation rule: only if a sentiment feature earns a validated verdict does it get promoted into the regime model as a third emission dimension (return, volatility, sentiment) — at which point the states would sharpen from "calm/mid/stress" into moods like euphoric-calm (quiet tape, greedy crowd — the dangerous one) versus fearful-calm (quiet tape, scarred crowd — historically the best buying weather). Same walk-forward test decides if that richer model earns its keep.

The new-era summary

Data didn't change what investing is — it changed when you can know things. Funds pay millions to know earnings direction weeks early. Our version of that edge is humbler but real: know the regime, know the crowding, know which trends are confirmed by actual co-movement — and let every new data source, however trendy, stand trial in the graph like everything else.
