Glossary

Every term used in the dashboard and playbook, in plain English.
CAGR
Compound annual growth rate — what an investment actually earned per year once compounding is accounted for. The destination number.
Sharpe ratio
Average return divided by volatility (annualized). Return per unit of stress. Roughly: 0.5 mediocre, 0.8 decent, 1.0+ good for simple rules.
Max drawdown
Worst peak-to-trough decline. The journey number — it decides whether you'd actually have held on.
Volatility
How much returns swing, usually annualized standard deviation. ~15% is normal for stocks; 30%+ is storm territory.
Beta
How much a stock moves per 1% market move. Beta 2 ≈ doubles every market swing, both directions.
Correlation
How much two things move together (−1 to +1). Low-correlation assets are diversifiers; in crashes, most correlations lurch toward 1.
Regime
A persistent market 'mood' — calm, choppy, stressed. Regimes cluster, which is why risk is more predictable than return.
Hidden Markov model
A statistical model that assumes an unobservable state generates the data, and infers today's state from recent behavior. Our regime detector.
Walk-forward
Testing discipline where the model only ever uses data available at each historical moment. The difference between a backtest and a fairy tale.
Lookahead bias
Accidentally letting a backtest peek at the future (e.g., using a month's average price to trade that same month). The most common way backtests lie.
Credit spread / OAS
Extra yield lenders demand from risky borrowers over Treasuries. Widening spreads = 'borrowing is getting harder' = the classic pre-storm signal.
10-month moving average
Average of the last ~10 months of prices. Price above it = uptrend intact. Old (1930s), simple, and still the benchmark trend filter.
Base rate
What happens on average, ignoring all signals — here, stocks up ~64% of months, +1%/month. Every trade idea must beat this, not zero.
t-statistic
How many standard errors an effect is from zero. |t| > 2 is the conventional 'probably real' bar. Small samples almost never clear it — respect that.
Hit rate
Percent of months a condition saw positive returns. 64% base rate is why shorting is structurally hard.
Conjunction
Two signals required together (credit stress AND broken trend). Conjunctions are where real edges usually hide — single indicators are too crowded.
Drawdown insurance
Giving up expected return to shrink worst-case losses (what trend/regime filters actually do). Insurance costs premium; the premium here is ~2.4% CAGR.
Equity risk premium
Expected stock return minus safe bond yield — the payment for bearing risk. Near zero today, which is what 'expensive market' means in one number.
Transition matrix
The regime model's table of switching odds: given today's mood, the probability of each mood tomorrow. Big diagonal numbers = moods are sticky = risk is forecastable.
Emission
What a hidden state 'shows' you — the daily return and volatility a regime generates. You infer the hidden mood from its visible emissions.
Baum–Welch (EM)
The fitting loop for a hidden Markov model: guess the states, sort days into them, re-measure each state, repeat until stable. No hand labels anywhere.
Forward algorithm
The daily Bayes update that turns yesterday's regime probabilities plus today's market move into today's regime probabilities. Cheap, online, no peeking ahead.
Correlation network
A graph where stocks are dots and strong return correlations are lines. The market's real seating chart — who actually trades together, whatever the sector label says.
Label propagation
Cluster discovery by peer pressure: every stock repeatedly adopts the most common group among its correlated neighbors until the map settles.
Long / flat / short
The three positions: own it, don't own it, bet against it. Long needs no forecast (drift pays you), flat needs a risk signal, short needs a direction edge — the rarest thing in markets.
Cluster migration
A stock changing which group it trades with — often the market repricing its story before the news says why. Worth investigating every time.
Prior / likelihood / posterior
The three beats of a Bayes update: what you believed this morning (prior), how well each possibility explains what you just saw (likelihood), what you believe now (posterior = prior × likelihood, rescaled).
Attention effect
Barber–Odean: retail investors buy whatever grabbed their attention; the surge overshoots, and returns after attention spikes are below average. Spikes are closer to sell signals.
Crowding
How one-sided the positioning in a trade has become. Crowded trades aren't wrong — they're fragile: everyone heads for the same exit at once.
Alternative data
Anything that estimates fundamentals before they're reported: card panels, satellite images, app downloads, scraped sentiment. The modern arms race in one phrase.
Terms get added as new nodes and edges enter the graph.
